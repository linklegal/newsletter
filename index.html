Newsletter | April 2020
This newsletter aims to briefly discuss the key corporate law developments during the period of January 15, 2020 - April 30, 2020.
PART I - COVID-19 RELATED CORPORATE LAW DEVELOPMENTS
A. UPDATES ISSUED BY THE MINISTRY OF CORPORATE AFFAIRS (“MCA”)
Extension of last date for filing of form NFRA-2
The MCA, vide Circular No. 07/2020 dated March 05, 2020, had extended the time limit for filing of form NFRA-2, for the reporting period of financial year 2018-19, to 150 (one hundred and fifty) days from the date of deployment of the form on the website of National Financial Reporting Authority (“NFRA”), i.e. 150 (one hundred and fifty) days from December 09, 2019.

In continuation of the aforesaid, the MCA, vide Circular No. 19/2020 dated April 30, 2020, has further extended the time limit for filing of Form NFRA-2 from 150 (one hundred and fifty) days to 210 (two hundred and ten) days from December 09, 2019.
Extension of last date for the existing independent directors (“IDs”) to apply for registration/listing in online data bank
The MCA had notified the Companies (Appointment and Qualification of Directors) Amendment Rules, 2020 on February 28, 2020 pursuant to which inter alia the time limit for the existing IDs to apply for registration/listing in online data bank had been extended from 3 (three) months to 5 (five) months.

In continuation of the aforesaid, the MCA has now notified the Companies (Appointment and Qualification of Directors) Rules, 2020 on April 29, 2020, pursuant to which the time limit for the existing IDs to apply for registration/listing in online data bank has been extended from 5 (five) months to 7 (seven) months.
Extension of period for names reserved and re-submission of forms
Under the provisions of the Companies Act, 2013 (“Act”), once a name is made available for a company for incorporation, such name is reserved for a period of 20 (twenty) days and in case of change in name of an existing company, the name is reserved for a period of 60 (sixty) days. Further, in case of a limited liability partnership (“LLP”), the name made available in case of incorporation or in case of change of name of an existing LLP, is reserved for a period of 90 (ninety) days. On April 22, 2020 the MCA extended the validity of all approved names, whether in case of incorporation of new companies/ LLPs and in case of change of name of existing companies/ LLPs for period of resubmission of forms for companies and LLP, whose validity was expiring on any day between March 15, 2020 to May 03, 2020 by additional 20 (twenty) days beyond May 03, 2020.

In addition to the aforesaid, where the Service Request Numbers were already marked under NTBR (not to be taken on record), extension would be provided on case to case basis.
Relaxation in holding of annual general meetings by companies whose financial year ended on December 31, 2019
The MCA, vide Circular No. 18/2020 dated April 21, 2020, has permitted companies whose financial year (other than first financial year) ended on December 31, 2019, to hold their annual general meetings (“AGMs”) within a period of 9 (nine) months from the closure of the financial year (i.e. September 30, 2020) without being in breach of the provisions of the Act.
Clarification on passing of ordinary and special resolutions and convening of extraordinary general meetings (“EGMs”) through video conferencing (“VC”) or other audio-visual conferencing (“OAVM”)
The MCA has, vide Circular No. 14/2020 dated April 08, 2020 and Circular No. 17/2020 dated April 13, 2020, permitted conduct of EGMs by companies via VC or OAVM and provided clarification in relation to passing of ordinary and special resolutions, respectively; provided that such companies comply with the prescribed requirements. Please refer to our update in relation to Circular 14/2020 and Circular 17/2020 here.
Board meetings through video conferencing
The MCA, vide notification dated March 19, 2020, has notified the Companies (Meetings of Board and its Powers) Amendment Rules, 2020 according to which companies have now been permitted to conduct board meetings to discuss matters provided under section 173 (2) of the Act read with rule 4 of the Companies (Meetings of Board and its Powers) Rules, 2014 including approval of the annual financial statements, Board’s report, approval of prospectus, the audit committee meetings for consideration of accounts, approval of matters relating to amalgamation, merger, demerger, acquisition and takeover, through video conferencing or other audio visual means. Prior to the amendment such matters were permitted to be discussed only at physical board meetings.
CSR activities relating to Covid-19
The MCA, vide an office memorandum dated March 28, 2020, has clarified that any contribution made by a company to the Government of India’s Prime Minister’s Citizen Assistance and Relief in Emergency Situations Fund (“PM-CARES Fund”), would qualify as such company’s corporate social responsibility (“CSR”) expenditure under the Act.

In addition to the said office memorandum, the MCA, vide Circular No. 15/2020 dated April 10, 2020, has released FAQs on eligibility of CSR expenditure related to Covid-19 which would qualify as a CSR expenditure under the Act. The FAQs may be referred to here.
Companies Fresh Start Scheme 2020 and LLP Settlement Scheme 2020
The MCA, vide Circular No. 12/2020 dated March 30, 2020, has introduced the Companies Fresh Start Scheme, 2020, which permits companies to file belated documents in the MCA-21 registry without any sanctions with regard to any additional fees for such delayed filings and grants immunity to such companies from prosecution or proceedings for imposing penalty on account of such belated filings. Similarly, the LLP Settlement Scheme, 2020, which was introduced pursuant to Circular No. 06/ 20 dated March 3, 2020 to reduce compliance burden on Limited Liability Partnerships in light of the Covid-19 pandemic, was amended vide Circular No. 13/2020 dated March 30, 2020.

Schemes act as one-time waivers by facilitating defaulting companies and LLPs to make a fresh start as a fully complaint entity by regularising past defaults in filings, irrespective of the duration of such defaults. Both the schemes are applicable with effect from April 1, 2020 and will continue till September 30, 2020. The pressing highlight of both the schemes is that they significantly reduce the financial burden on entities which could otherwise be saddled with considerable financial penalty, especially if here are long-standing filing defaults.

In addition to the above, the MCA, has also allowed Director Identification Number (“DIN”) holders of DINs marked as ‘Deactivated’ due to non-filing of DIR-3KYC/DIR-3 KYC-Web and those companies whose compliance status has been marked as “ACTIVE non-compliant” due to non-filing of Active Company Tagging Identities and Verification (ACTIVE) e-form, to comply with the necessary filings with no additional fees till September 30, 2020.
Amendment to the minimum default amount under Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code 2016 (“IBC”)
Under provisions of the IBC, a corporate insolvency resolution process could earlier be triggered against a company if there is a minimum default by the company of an amount of INR 1 lakh. However, the MCA, vide notification S.O. 1205(E) dated March 24, 2020, has increased the threshold amount to INR 1 crore. Hence, now an insolvency petition can only be initiated against a company if such company defaults in payment of an amount equal to or more than INR 1 crore.
Special measures under the Act and Limited Liability Partnership Act, 2008 (“LLP Act”)
The MCA, vide Circular No. 11/2020 dated March 24, 2020, has implemented the following special measures with a view to support and enable companies and LLPs to focus on taking necessary measures to address the Covid-19 threat and reduce their compliance burden and other risks:
  1. No additional fees to be charged for late filing during a moratorium period from April 01, 2020 to September 30, 2020, in respect of any document, return, statement etc., required to be filed, irrespective of its due date.
  2. The mandatory requirement of holding meetings of the board of directors of the companies within the prescribed interval of 120 (one hundred and twenty) days has been extended by a period of 60 (sixty) days till 30th September, 2020. Accordingly, as a one-time relaxation the gap between two consecutive meetings of the board of directors may extend to 180 (one hundred and eighty) days, instead of 120 (one hundred and twenty) days as required under the Act.
  3. According to the Act, in every financial year IDs are required to hold at least one meeting without the attendance of Non-Independent directors and members of management. If, however, the IDs have not been able to hold such a meeting for the financial year 2019-20, then this will not be a non-compliance under the Act.
  4. Requirement under section 73(2)(c) of the Act to create the deposit repayment reserve of 20% (twenty percent) of deposits maturing during the financial year 2020-21 before April 30, 2020 is now allowed to be complied with till June 30, 2020.
  5. Requirement under rule 18 of the Companies (Share Capital & Debentures) Rules, 2014 to invest or deposit 15% (fifteen percent) of debentures maturing during a particular year in specified instruments before April 30, 2020, is now allowed to be fulfilled on or before June 30, 2020.
  6. Newly incorporated companies are required to file a declaration for Commencement of Business within 180 (one hundred and eighty) days of incorporation under section 10A of the Act. An additional period of 180 (one hundred and eighty) days has been allowed for this compliance.
  7. Non-compliance of minimum residency in India for a period of at least 182 (one hundred and eighty two) days by at least one director of every company, as required under Section 149 of the Act, would not be treated as a non-compliance for the financial year 2019-20.
  8. The Companies (Auditor's Report) Order, 2020, which is aimed at enhancing the overall quality of reporting by the company auditors, to be made applicable from the financial year 2020-21 instead of being applicable from the financial year 2019-2020 as notified earlier.
B. UPDATES ISSUED BY SECURITIES AND EXCHANGE BOARD OF INDIA (“SEBI”)
Relaxation in Regulation 24(i)(f) of the SEBI (Buy-back of Securities) Regulations, 2018 due to the Covid-19 pandemic
Regulation 24(i)(f) of SEBI (Buy-back of Securities) Regulations, 2018 prohibits a company from raising further capital for a period of 1 (one) year from the expiry of buyback period, except in discharge of their subsisting obligations. However, to enable companies to raise funds and to have relatively quicker access to capital, SEBI, vide Circular No. SEBI/HO/CFD/DCR2/CIR/P/2020/69 dated April 23, 2020, has reduced the aforesaid period of 1 (one) year to 6 (six) months. This relaxation is applicable till December 31, 2020.
Relaxation in relation to Regulation 44(5) of the SEBI (Listing Obligations and Disclosure Requirements) Regulations, 2015 (“LODR Regulations”)
In line with the MCA Circular No. 18/2020 dated April 21, 2020 which had permitted companies, whose financial year (other than first financial year) ended on December 31, 2019 to hold their AGMs on or before September 30, 2020 without being in breach of the provisions of the Companies Act, 2013, SEBI, vide Circular No. SEBI/HO/CFD/CMD1/CIR/P/2020/71 dated April 23, 2020, has relaxed regulation 44(5) of the LODR Regulations, whereby the top 100 (one hundred) listed entities by market capitalization whose financial year ended on December 31, 2019 have been permitted to hold their AGMs within a period of 9 (nine) months from the closure of the financial year (i.e., by September 30, 2020).
Relaxations from certain provisions of SEBI (Issue of Capital and Disclosure Requirements) Regulations, 2018 (“ICDR Regulations”)
In order to ease the conditions relating to raising of funds from the securities market vide public issue/rights issue, SEBI, vide Circular No. SEBI/HO/CFD/DIL1/CIR/P/2020/66 and Circular No. SEBI/HO/CFD/CIR/CFD/DIL/67/2020, each dated April 21, 2020, has granted temporary one-time relaxations from several provisions of the ICDR Regulations.

A summary of these relaxations along with the press release issued by SEBI on April 21, 2020 pertaining to measures to further facilitate fund raising from capital markets in the backdrop of Covid-19 pandemic has been discussed in detail here.

Relaxation from compliance with certain provisions of the SEBI (Substantial Acquisition of Shares and Takeovers) Regulations, 2011 (“SAST Regulations”)
The disclosure filings under regulations 30(1) and 30(2) {Continual disclosures} and 31(4) {Disclosure of encumbered shares} of the SAST Regulations, require the shareholders to compile, collate and disseminate information of their consolidated shareholding as on March 31, 2020 to the company and the stock exchanges within 7 (seven) working days from the end of the financial year. These reports as per the 2020 calendar were required to be filed by the entities by April 15, 2020.

SEBI, vide Circular No. SEBI/HO/CFD/DCR1/CIR/P/2020/49 dated March 27, 2020, has extended the due date of filing such disclosures till June 01, 2020.
REALISATION AND REPARTIATION OF EXPORT PROCEEDS
RBI, vide RBI/2019-20/206 A. P. (DIR Series) Circular No. 27 dated April 01, 2020, has increased the time period prescribed for realisation and repatriation of export proceeds representing the full export value of goods or software or services exported, from the existing period of 9 (nine) months to 15 (fifteen) months from the date of export, for all exports made up to or on July 31, 2020.

PART II - NON COVID-19 RELATED CORPORATE LAW DEVELOPMENTS
A. UPDATES UNDER THE ACT
MCA notifies the provisions relating to takeover of unlisted companies
The MCA, vide notification dated February 3, 2020, has notified that the provisions of sub-sections (11) and (12) of Section 230 of the Act, which pertain to takeover of an unlisted company through a scheme of arrangement placed before the National Company Law Tribunal (“NCLT”). The MCA has also amended the Companies (Compromises, Arrangements and Amalgamations) Rules, 2016 and the National Company Law Tribunal Rules, 2016 as a consequence of notifying sub-sections (11) and (12) of Section 230 of the Act.

Key highlights of the notified sections and rules are as follows:
  1. A member or members of an unlisted company acting together, holding not less than 3/4th of the shares in the company, can make an application to the NCLT to buy out the minority shareholders holding the remaining shares.

    The term “shares” has been defined to mean equity shares with voting rights including any securities, such as depository receipts, which entitles the holder to exercise voting rights.

    Though the rules do not explicitly provide an obligation on the part of the offeree minority shareholders to mandatorily sell their shares, the scheme allows the shareholders with a 75% shareholding to acquire the remaining minority shares, thereby intending a squeeze out of the minority shareholders.
     
  2. Such application is required to be accompanied by a report from a registered valuer disclosing the valuation of the shares after considering certain prescribed factors.
     
  3. Prior to making such an application, the company is required to open a separate bank account and deposit an amount not less than ½ of total consideration of the proposed offer.
MCA eases the process for incorporation of companies and introduces new e-forms ‘SPICE+’ and ‘AGILE PRO’
The MCA, vide Notification No. G.S.R. 128(E) dated February 18, 2020, has amended the Companies (Incorporation) Rules, 2014 to further simplify incorporation of companies w.e.f. February 23, 2020.

As part of the ease of doing business initiatives of the Government, the MCA has introduced a new web-based Form SPICe+ (SPICe Plus), by replacing the existing Form SPICe for incorporation of companies. This will be applicable for all new companies incorporated on or after February 23, 2020. The Form SPICe+ is an integrated single window web form that enables application for name reservation, incorporation of company and DIN, PAN and TAN allotment, EPFO registration etc. all under one form.

Further, the existing AGILE form has been renamed as form AGILE-PRO, which enables application to be made for Profession Tax registration and opening of bank account at the time of incorporation of a company. While applying for the aforesaid registration is mandatory at the time of incorporation, users can also choose to apply for the registration of Goods and Service Tax Identification Number.

The new form SPICE+ is a welcome step as it aims to streamline and expedite the incorporation process. The new form will enable on screen filing and real time data validation for seamless incorporation of companies. It is proposed to have a user-friendly dashboard for the front office incorporation application.
MCA provides clarifications on liability of IDs, non-promoters and non-key managerial personnel, non-executive directors
The MCA, vide Circular No. 01/2020 dated March 2, 2020, issued a standard operating procedure (“SOP”) to all the Registrar of Companies (“RoC”), Regional Directors and Official Liquidators for prosecution or internal adjudication proceedings initiated against the IDs and non-executive directors not being promoter or key managerial personnel (“NEDs”) in respect of non-compliance with the provisions of the Act. The MCA, vide this circular inter-alia clarifies that IDs and NEDs should not be held liable or penalized for non-compliance of the provisions of the Act unless the defaults/offences have occurred with their knowledge or with their consent or by the reason of them not acting diligently and brings in the concept that “officers in default” shall be such persons who are responsible for the day-to-day management of the companies to whom specific matters or authority has been given.

The SOP aims to streamline the process of prosecuting IDs/NEDs. The said circular inter-alia provides that for all ongoing cases or cases where proceedings are to be initiated, the registrars should follow the below standard operating procedures:
  1. To ascertain the nature of default in the company;
  2. To ascertain who are the officers in default in the company, necessary documents may be sought at the time of serving notices for inquiry or investigation, for ascertaining the officers involved;
  3. To ascertain serving director or KMP as on the date of default, by examining the records available with the Registrar, including e-Forms DIR-11 or DIR-12, Annual Returns, Financial Statements to ascertain whether a particular director or KMP was serving in the company on the date of  default;
  4. Flings of information and records with the RoC, maintenance of statutory and secretarial records, compliance with the orders of statutory authorities to be responsibility of the WTDs and the KMPs. The responsibility of NEDs ordinarily may arise only in case there is no WTD/KMP;
  5. Where lapses are attributable to the decisions taken by the Board or its committees, all care must be taken to ensure that civil /criminal proceedings are not initiated against the IDs and NEDs unless there is sufficient evidence; and
  6. To seek guidance from MCA on proceedings in case of any doubts with regard to the liability of any person, for proceedings to be initiated by the RoC, guidance may be sought from MCA (through the office of the Director General of Corporate Affairs). Consequently, any such proceedings must be initiated after receiving due sanction from the MCA.
MCA has directed all registrars to implement the SOP with immediate effect, including in respect of ongoing cases. Further, with respect to the cases which are already under prosecution, but which do not meet the criteria mentioned in the circular, then those cases should be submitted to MCA for necessary examination and further direction thereon.
Link Legal Insight:
The SOP clarifies that the imposition of liability of the IDs and NEDs for various violations of the Act and acts as a restriction on the powers of the Registrars thus safeguarding the IDs and NEDs. However, the said SOP does not provide a blanket protection to the IDs and NEDs and the same  needs to be assessed on a case to case basis. It has been prescribed that all care must be taken to ensure that civil or criminal proceedings are not unnecessarily initiated against the IDs or the NEDs unless sufficient evidence exists to the contrary. The SOP is a principle-based approach and requires registrars to understand the nature of default first and casts a greater responsibility and obligations to examine relevant information on the RoCs while dealing with any proceedings against them.

Though the said clarification is a welcome step, the position of IDs and NEDs is still unclear under other statutes where there is no change or relaxation in terms of liability that can be imposed, under other statutes (Income Tax Act, 1961, the Prevention of Money Laundering Act, 2002, environmental legislations, the SEBI, etc.) and the IDs and NEDs are still exposed to the risk of liability as these statutes do not carve-out such exceptions for IDs/NEDs by virtue of their role in the company.
MCA allows Insolvency Professional(s) to undertake filings under the Act
The MCA, vide Circular No. 08/2020 dated March 6, 2020, has provided clarity in respect of statutory compliances for companies undergoing corporate resolution process under the IBC.

According to the said circular, an insolvency professional {interim resolution professional (IRP) or resolution professional (RP) or liquidator} appointed under IBC is to be responsible for filing all e-forms in the MCA portal and sign the form in the capacity of CEO in order to meet the filing protocol. Such filings can be made by the insolvency professional after following the procedure mentioned in the circular.
MCA notifies that Section 460 regarding condonation of delay also applicable to LLPs
Section 460 of the Act provides that where a company does not make an application required to be made to the Central Government or does not file any document with the RoC as required, within the time prescribed for making such application or filing such document, the Central Government has the power to condone such delay. The MCA, vide notification dated January 30, 2020, has made the provisions of Section 460 applicable to LLPs with effect from January 30, 2020.
Link Legal Insight:
Every LLP is required to make annual filings with the RoC in Form 8 (Statement of Account and Solvency within a period of 30 days from the end of 6 months of the financial year) and  Form-11 (Annual Return to be filed within 60 days of close of its financial year). LLPs are also required to make event- based filings such as change of registered office, change of name of LLP, change in LLP agreement, etc. with the RoC. As per the earlier rules, in case of delay in filing of annual forms (i.e. Form 8 and Form 11), a penalty of INR 100 per day per form was payable from the due date of filing return till the date the actual return is filed by the LLP. There was no power given to the Central Government to condone any such delay. However, pursuant to the above notification, LLPs can now approach the Central Government to condone the delay in making relevant applications or filings.
MCA notifies the Companies (Winding Up) Rules, 2020
The MCA, vide notification dated January 24, 2020, has introduced the Companies (Winding Up) Rules, 2020 with effect from April 1, 2020. The most significant provision in the rues is that it provides for summary winding up procedure for the following companies:
  1. Companies which have taken deposit and total outstanding deposits does not exceed Rs. 25 lakhs;
  2. Companies of which the total outstanding loan including secured loan does not exceed Rs. 50 lakhs;
  3. Companies of which turnover does not exceed Rs. 50 crores; or
  4. Companies of which paid up capital does not exceed Rs. 1 crore.
While the jurisdiction for winding up of small companies of the nature listed above has been shifted to the liquidator instead of the National Company Law Tribunal, which is a welcome move to reduce the burden of the courts, in the absence of specific provisions in relation to timelines around this procedure, it is not certain whether this summary winding up provision will act as a fast track process for small companies.

B. UPDATES UNDER FEMA
100% Foreign Direct Investment allowed in Insurance Intermediaries
The Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade has issued Press Note No. 1 (2020 series) (“Press Note”) dated February 21, 2020 amending the consolidated Foreign Direct Investment Policy of 2017 (“FDI Policy”) by allowing 100% Foreign Direct Investment (“FDI”) in insurance intermediaries. The Department of Economic Affairs has thereafter issued a notification amending the relevant provisions of the Foreign Exchange Management (Non-debt Instruments) Rules, 2019 on April 27, 2020 (“Notification”) bringing the Press Note into effect.

Insurance intermediaries include entities such as insurance brokers, re-insurance brokers, insurance consultants, corporate agents, third party administrators, surveyors, loss assessors, and such other entities as may be notified by the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India (“IRDAI”). However, FDI in insurance companies continues to be at 49% under the automatic route.

The Notification, however, requires insurance intermediaries that have majority shareholding of foreign investors to undertake the following:
  1. be incorporated as a limited company under the Act;
     
  2. at least one from among the chairman of the board of directors or the chief executive officer or principal officer or managing director of the insurance intermediary is required to be a resident Indian citizen;
     
  3. is required to take prior permission of IRDAI for repatriating dividend;
     
  4. is required to bring in the latest technological, managerial, and other skills;
     
  5. is not permitted to make payments to the foreign group or promoter or subsidiary or interconnected or associate entities beyond what is necessary or permitted by IRDAI;
     
  6. is required to make disclosures in the formats to be specified by IRDAI of all payments made to its group or promoter or subsidiary or interconnected or associate entities; and
     
  7. is required to ensure that the composition of the board of directors and key management personnel meet the requirements specified by the concerned regulators.
The Notification has also clarified that where an entity like a bank whose primary business is outside the insurance area, is allowed by IRDAI to function as an insurance intermediary, the foreign equity investment caps applicable in that sector shall continue to apply, subject to the condition that the revenues of such entities from the primary (non-insurance related) business must remain above 50 per cent of their total revenues in any financial year.
Link Legal Insight:
Opening up of FDI in the insurance intermediaries’ sector up to 100% under the automatic will attract foreign players as well as bring in technical and managerial skills as prescribed under the Notification. However, the other conditions such as obtaining approval from the IRDAI for repatriation of dividends and related party payments will test the appetite of such foreign players and their willingness to be subjugated to such restrictions.
Foreign Exchange Rules prescribe adherence to pricing guidelines on acquisition after renunciation in a rights issue
Under the Foreign Exchange Management (Non-debt Instruments) Rules, 2019 a person resident outside India was allowed to make investments in equity instruments (other than share warrants) issued by an Indian company on a rights issue basis that was renounced by the person to whom it was offered, at the same price and on the same terms on which the rights shares were offered to the shareholders of the company renouncing the offer.

The Notification (April 27, 2020, mentioned at serial no. 1 above) has inserted a new provision 7A in the Foreign Exchange Management (Non-debt Instruments) Rules, 2019 (“Rules”) according to which a person resident outside India who has acquired a right to acquire equity instruments (other than share warrants) of an Indian company from a person resident in India who has renounced his right to acquire such equity instruments pursuant to a rights offer by the company, may acquire such equity instruments (other than share warrants) against the said rights in accordance with the pricing guidelines specified under rule 21 of the Rules. In other words, such equity instruments can be acquired by the person resident outside India at a price which cannot not be less than:
  1. in case of a listed Indian company or in case of a company going through a delisting process as per the SEBI (Delisting of Equity Shares) Regulations, 2009, the price worked out in accordance with the SEBI guidelines; or
  2. in case of an unlisted Indian Company, the valuation of equity instruments done as per any internationally accepted pricing methodology for valuation on an arm’s length basis duly certified by a Chartered Accountant or a Merchant Banker registered with the SEBI or a practicing Cost Accountant.
Foreign Exchange Rules amended to prevent opportunistic takeovers of Indian companies from countries sharing a border with India
The Foreign Exchange Management (Non-debt Instruments) Rules, 2019, had recently been amended vide notification of the Foreign Exchange Management (Non-debt Instruments) Amendment Rules, 2020 issued by the Department of Economic Affairs on April 22, 2020 (“April Notification”).

The April Notification states that an entity of a country sharing land border with India or where the beneficial owner of an investment into India is situated in or is a citizen of any such country, will be permitted to invest in India through the FDI route only on receiving prior approval from the Government of India.

The above conditions would apply to the following countries which share a land border with India: (a) Pakistan; (b) Bangladesh; (c) China; (d) Myanmar; (e) Bhutan; (f) Afghanistan; and (h) Nepal.

The April Notifications also provides that in the event of any transfer of ownership of any existing or future FDI in an entity in India, directly or indirectly, resulting in the beneficial ownership being transferred to a person who is a resident of the above mentioned countries or an entity incorporated in the countries specified above would also require prior approval from the Government of India.
Link Legal Insight:
The conditions prescribed under the April Notification are similar to such protectionist measures adopted by other countries which seek to curb opportunistic takeovers especially from China in the wake of the COVID-19 impact on the economy. However, the April Notification does not provide any clarity if the calculation of beneficial interest will be as per the Act or the Prevention of Money Laundering Act, 2002.

Further, the April Notification does not clarify the status of investments from Hong Kong, which is a special administrative region of China. Current and future investors which have ties to Hong Kong may need to seek clarification from the Indian government on this issue.

The government approval route is quite a long drawn process and one has to see on how the government will handle expediting these approvals due to the large presence of Chinese investors in the country.

Clarification on FDI Policy on Single Brand Retail Trading for goods sourced from Special Economic Zones
As per the extant FDI Policy, entities with FDI over 51% (fifty one percent) in the single brand retail trading (“SBRT”) sector are required to comply with local sourcing norms wherein the SBRT entity must ensure that 30% (thirty percent) of the value of goods procured are sourced from India.

The Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade has now issued a clarification on February 27, 2020 according to which sourcing of goods from Special Economic Zones (“SEZs”) would qualify as goods sourced from India for the purpose of 30% mandatory sourcing from India, subject to Special Economic Zones Act, 2005 (as amended from time to time) and other applicable laws, rules and regulations; provided, however, that the goods proposed to be sourced by an SBRT entity from such units located in SEZs should be manufactured in India.
C. UPDATES ISSUED BY SEBI
SEBI eases registration requirements under the FPI Regulations
SEBI, vide Notification No. SEBI/LAD-NRO/GN/2020/09 dated April 7, 2020 (“Notification”), has amended Regulation 5(a)(iv) of the SEBI (Foreign Portfolio Investors) Regulations, 2019 (“FPI Regulations”), pursuant to which entities from countries that are members of the Financial Action Task Force (“FATF”) as well as from any country specified by the Central Government are eligible to register as Category I foreign portfolio investors (“FPI”). Prior to this notification only entities from countries that were members of the FATF were eligible to register as Category I FPIs.

On February 21, 2020, the FATF issued a list of jurisdictions which are under increased monitoring (also referred to as the grey list), which included Mauritius. It also stated that jurisdictions under increased monitoring are actively working with the FATF to address strategic deficiencies in their regimes to counter money laundering, terrorist financing, and proliferation financing and when the FATF places a jurisdiction under increased monitoring, it means the country has committed to resolve swiftly the identified strategic deficiencies within agreed timeframes and is subject to increased monitoring.

Following on from the Notification, the Department of Economic Affairs, Ministry of Finance vide order dated April 13, 2020 has specified Mauritius as an eligible country for the purpose of registration as a Category I FPI, in response to the FATF categorizing it under the grey list on February 21, 2020.
Link Legal Insight:
It appears that the Notification to amend the FPI Regulations was brought in to nullify the effect on the FPI Regulations caused by FATF listing Mauritius in the grey list on so that entities from Mauritius can register as Category I FPI and accordingly avail all the benefits that accompany such registration it.
SEBI streamlines Rights Issue process
In order to make the process efficient and effective, SEBI has streamlined certain aspects of rights issue process by amending the SEBI (Issue of Capital and Disclosure Requirements) Regulations, 2018 ("ICDR Regulations") and LODR Regulations, vide notification no. SEBI/HO/CFD/DIL2/CIR/P/2020/13 dated January 22, 2020. The said amendments will not only reduce the timelines but will also provide clarity on the renunciation and trading of rights entitlements.

Mandatory ASBA
Earlier, renouncees were required to invest only through the non-ASBA process which also involved physical verification of forms. Now, all applications in rights issues are mandatorily required to be done through the ASBA process.

Record date and advertisement
Advance notice for record date has been reduced from 7 (seven) working days (excluding the date of intimation and the record date) to 3 (three) working days. Further, issuance of newspaper advertisement disclosing date of completion of dispatch and intimation of same to the stock exchanges for dissemination on their websites, as per Regulation 84 (1) of ICDR Regulations, is now required to be completed by the issuer at least 2 (two) days before the date of opening of the issue.

Dematerialized Rights Entitlement
The concept of dematerialized rights entitlement has been introduced. Companies undertaking a rights issue are mandatorily required to credit the rights entitlement to shareholders in demat form. SEBI has also introduced a legal framework for renunciation of rights entitlement and has allowed trading of such rights entitlement on the stock exchanges or transfer through off-market transactions, confirming the existing market practice. Depositories and stock exchanges have been directed to provide necessary procedure in this regard. Companies are required to factor in time for those shareholders, who own shares in physical form, to open demat accounts.

However, credit of rights entitlement to the demat account of shareholders who are resident in other jurisdictions will require detailed analysis in view of offering restrictions applicable under securities laws of other countries. In view of the existing practice of reading laws of various jurisdictions harmoniously and the global practice for rights offerings, crediting rights entitlements to shareholders who are resident outside India will require further discussion.

SEBI has also clarified that the investors will not be allowed to withdraw their application after the issue closing date.
Link Legal Insight:
This is a welcome change by SEBI and the notification will not only reduce the timelines but will also provide clarity on the renunciation and trading of rights entitlements.
